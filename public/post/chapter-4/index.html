Article 4

April 12, 2017

In 2000, the Chilean government introduced Intercultural Bilingual Education, which recognised the need for Mapuche histories and knowledge to be taught and promoted within the official curriculum and educational system. Whilst acknowledging the benefits of Intercultural Bilingual Education, Webb and Radcliffe criticise the way in which this has been implemented. The current focus on assimilation of Mapuche individuals into “Chilean society” does not take into account the very different historical experiences between Mapuche communities and those of European descent in Chile, and ignores the impact that this has on social, economic and educational opportunities. IBE is insufficient in its inclusion of Mapuche language, which is always seen as secondary to Spanish and often to English, and Mapuche history, which is often represented either as an “alternative” narrative or told from a Western, white, or colonial perspective. Mapuche histories are presented as “Other”, or as an addition to an official curriculum that centers on the history, language, sciences and knowledge of a white Chilean society. Access to higher education under a neoliberal multicultural system is severely affected by socio-economic factors, and even more so by ethno-national factors, an issue that has been brought to the forefront of recent protests in Chile, including the 2019 protests, the 2011 Chilean “Winter of Discontent”, and, to a lesser extent, the 2006 “Penguin Revolution”. All three of these major uprisings in Chile over the last 20 years were initially sparked by youth movements, more specifically, by secondary school students, and Mapuche youth groups and individuals have played an enormous part in this. Although the triggers for these uprisings have generally centered more generally on the rising prices of transport and education, two issues that affect Mapuche students more than any other demographic in Chile’s urban areas, they all broadened their demands to include a general rejection of the central government’s policies of neoliberalism, privatisation and commodification of education. Groups such as Meli Newen, an organisation of secondary school students formed during the 2006 protests to coordinate indigenous action across Chile, played a key role in the implementation of the 2008 General Education Law which, despite its limitations, succeeded in introducing IBE and began a process of increased inclusion of Mapuche education into the general curriculum.
